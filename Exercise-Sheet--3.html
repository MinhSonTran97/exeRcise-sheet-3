pixar_films <- read_csv("data/pixar_films.csv") %>%

The possible film_rating values are G, PG, N/A:

  • ‚ÄúG‚Äù: General audiences

  • ‚ÄúPG‚Äù: Parental guidance is advised

  • ‚ÄúN/A‚Äù: Film Rating is not available / no rating provided. This is included since we only removed entries with missing titles.

pixar_films <- pixar_films %>%
  mutate(film_rating = factor(film_rating, levels = c("G", "PG"), ordered = TRUE))

Converting film_rating into rating_factor is appropriate because:

  • film_rating represents a set of discrete categories, not numeric values. These categories describe the type of audience the film is suitable for, not a measurable quantity.

  • Treating film_rating as a factor helps R recognize it as a categorical variable, which allow us to better summarize, plot, and handle the data in modeling.

Research question: How has the global literacy rate changed between 1970 and 2015?

The period from 1970 to 2015 includes major global education initiatives, such as UNESCO‚Äôs Education for All, making it an interesting and relevant timeframe to assess how these efforts have impacted the average literacy rate worldwide.

e)

world_literacy <- read_csv("data/Cross-country literacy rates - World Bank, CIA World Factbook, and other sources.csv")

literacy_trend <- world_literacy %>%
  filter(Year >= 1970) %>%
  group_by(Year) %>%
  summarise(avg_world_literacy = mean(`Literacy rates (World Bank, CIA World Factbook, and other sources)`), na.rm = TRUE)

literacy_change <- literacy_trend %>%
  filter(Year %in% c(1970, 2015)) %>%
  mutate(change = round(avg_world_literacy - lag(avg_world_literacy), 1)) %>%
  ungroup()

The global mean literacy rate rose from 76.03% in 1970 to 84.07% in 2015, an increase of about 8% over 45 years. While the increase may seem modest, it represents millions of additional literate people globally, which is a significant improvement, especially in regions starting with lower literacy.
